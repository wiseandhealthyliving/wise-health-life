The Herbal Glossary

Defining the science behind the plants.

A B C D H L R T
A
Adaptogen Plants that help the body respond to stress, anxiety, and fatigue by regulating the HPA Axis. Example: Ashwagandha, Rhodiola.
Antispasmodic A substance used to relieve or prevent muscle spasms, particularly in the digestive or respiratory tracts. Example: Thyme, Peppermint.
B
Bioavailability The proportion of a substance that enters the circulation when introduced into the body and is able to have an active effect.
E
Enzymatic Inhibition When one substance (like Piperine) temporarily "distracts" a liver enzyme so another substance (like Curcumin) can slip past.
H
HPA Axis The Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal axis; our central stress response system that controls cortisol levels.
P
Pharmacokinetics The study of how the body moves a substance through absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion.
R
Ricinoleic Acid A unique fatty acid found in castor oil that provides anti-inflammatory and lymphatic-stimulating effects.
T
Terpenes Aromatic compounds found in many plants that contribute to their scent and therapeutic benefits (e.g., Limonene in lemons).
Thermogenesis The process of heat production in organisms, often stimulated by certain herbs to aid metabolism. Example: Ginger.

Safety & Interactions

While herbal synergy can enhance wellness, it is vital to remember that "more effective" also means "more potent." Please consider the following:

  • The "Grapefruit Effect": Just as black pepper increases absorption, certain compounds can inhibit enzymes that metabolize prescription medications, leading to dangerously high levels in the bloodstream.
  • Blood Thinners: Pairings involving Cayenne, Garlic, or Ginger can enhance the effects of anticoagulant medications (like Warfarin or Aspirin).
  • Consult a Professional: If you are pregnant, nursing, or taking maintenance medication for blood pressure or diabetes, always consult your healthcare provider before introducing concentrated herbal pairings.
